# Personal Finance Curriculum Research
## 1,000-Lesson Duolingo-Style Learning Path

> **Purpose:** Define the complete learning path for 50 units x 20 lessons = 1,000 lessons covering personal finance from absolute beginner to expert mastery.

---

## Design Philosophy

### Core Principles
1. **Start absurdly simple.** Lesson 1 asks "What is money?" — no assumed knowledge whatsoever.
2. **One concept per lesson.** Each lesson teaches exactly one atomic idea (e.g., "what is a checking account" is separate from "what is a savings account").
3. **Scaffolding.** Every lesson builds on the previous. No concept appears without its prerequisite already taught.
4. **Spaced review.** Every 5th lesson within a unit is a review/practice lesson that reinforces the previous 4 lessons.
5. **Real-world anchoring.** Abstract concepts are always grounded in relatable scenarios (pizza, coffee, paychecks, road trips).
6. **Progressive complexity.** The same topic (e.g., "budgeting") appears at multiple tiers with increasing depth.
7. **Engagement hooks.** Lessons use varied question types, visual aids, and scenario-based problems to prevent monotony.

### Anti-Dropout Design
People quit learning finance because:
- **Too abstract** --> Every lesson uses concrete dollar amounts and real scenarios
- **Too much jargon** --> Terms introduced one at a time with plain-English definitions first
- **Feels irrelevant** --> Lessons connect to life events (first job, renting, buying a car)
- **Information overload** --> Strict one-concept-per-lesson rule
- **No sense of progress** --> Frequent review lessons give mastery feelings; XP rewards are generous early

### SVG Diagram Opportunities
Marked with [DIAGRAM] throughout. Best candidates:
- Pie charts for budget breakdowns
- Bar charts for comparing options
- Flowcharts for decision trees (should I rent or buy?)
- Timeline diagrams for compound interest growth
- Pyramid diagrams for financial priority hierarchy
- Thermometer/progress visuals for savings goals
- Paycheck stub breakdowns
- Tax bracket staircase diagrams
- Stock market candlestick/line chart basics
- Estate planning family tree diagrams

## Appendix C: Question Type Distribution Recommendations

For each 20-lesson unit, recommended question distribution:
- **Multiple Choice:** 50% (10 lessons predominantly MC)
- **True/False:** 20% (4 lessons predominantly T/F)
- **Fill-in-the-Blank / Word Bank:** 20% (4 lessons with fill-blank for key terms and formulas)
- **Review/Mixed:** 10% (2 lessons using all types)

Each lesson should have 6-10 questions for a total of ~120-200 questions per unit and ~6,000-10,000 questions across the entire course.

## Appendix D: Engagement & Review Cadence

- **Every 5th lesson:** Review lesson within each unit (reinforces previous 4 lessons)
- **Every 10th unit:** Checkpoint unit reviewing entire tier (Units 10, 20, 30, 40, 50)
- **Spaced repetition:** Review questions from earlier units should appear in later units' review lessons
- **Milestone celebrations:** End of each tier = XP bonus + achievement unlock
- **Scenario diversity:** Each unit should use at least 3 different real-world scenarios
- **Difficulty curve:** Lessons 1-5 in any unit are easier; 16-19 are hardest; 20 is comprehensive review

## Appendix E: Real-World Scenario Bank

Recurring characters and scenarios for continuity and relatability:
- **Alex** - Just graduated college, first job, student loans, wants to move out
- **Jordan** - Mid-career, married, new baby, buying a house
- **Sam** - Freelance graphic designer, irregular income, no benefits
- **Maria** - Single mom, two jobs, rebuilding credit after divorce
- **David & Kim** - Dual-income couple, different money styles, planning for retirement
- **Grandpa Ray** - Retired, fixed income, teaching grandkids about money
- **Tanya** - Small business owner, mixing personal and business finance
- **Marcus** - High earner, lifestyle inflation problem, late start on saving

These characters appear throughout the course, with their financial situations evolving as lessons progress. Learners develop empathy and see how concepts apply to different life situations.
